Colossal Cave Adventure (A.K.A. "ADVENT," "Colossal Cave," or "Adventure") was developed between 1975 and 1977 by Will Crowther in FORTRAN for the PDP-10 mainframe, and expanded in 1977 by Don Woods. This version was ported to DOS in 1990 by Kevin Black.

Adventure is the first known work of interactive fiction, the first text adventure game, and the precursor for the adventure game genre.

CYBIS

Download

Credit for this package goes to Tom Hunter, Paul Koning, Nat Kannan, and Gerard van der Grinten.

This package contains an emulator for CYBIS, running on top of NOS (an operating system), running on a DtCYBER (version 5.5.1) emulation of a CDC CYBER mainframe. Also included are usage guides for PLATO, CYBIS, and the TUTOR programming language, and a copy of Pterm version 5.0.8. All contents are available for Windows, macOS, and Linux.

PLATO (Programmed Logic for Automatic Teaching Operations) was the first generalized computer-assisted instruction system. Starting in 1960, it ran on the University of Illinois' ILLIAC I. By the late 1970s, PLATO supported several thousand graphics terminals worldwide. Pterm (PLATO terminal emulator) is an emulator for one of those graphics terminals, and is required to connect to the system. Many modern concepts were originally developed on PLATO, including forums, message boards, email, chat rooms, instant messaging, remote screen sharing, and multiplayer video games.

ILLIAC I was the first of the ILLIAC (ILLInois Automatic Computer) series of computers built in 1952 by the University of Illinois. ILLIAC I was the first computer built and owned entirely by a United States educational institution.

PLATO was developed at CERL (Computer-based Eduction Research Laboratory) at the University of Illinois. In 1976, CDC (Control Data Corporation) purchased the commercial rights to PLATO. The rights to the name were sold, and the product was rebranded to CYBIS (CYber-Based Instructional System).

Eventually, University Online (later VCampus) acquired CYBIS from CDC. The contents of this package became available after VCampus went out of business and its assets were transferred to its former CEO Nat Kannan.

TUTOR (A.K.A. "PLATO Author Language"), is a programming language developed for use on the PLATO system at the University of Illinois. TUTOR was initially designed by Paul Tenczar. Programs written in TUTOR are called "lessons," since the language was designed for computer assisted/managed instruction.

It is highly recommended that you use Pterm to connect to either Cyber1 or IRATA.ONLINE instead of your own emulation, so that you have access to the old lessons.

Dungeons of the Necromancer's Domain

Download

dnd (A.K.A. "The Game of Dungeons") was one of the first role-playing video games. The name is derived from the abbreviation "D&D" of the tabletop game Dungeons & Dragons, which released in 1974. dnd was developed by Gary Whisenhunt and Ray Wood in the TUTOR programming language for the PLATO system in 1974 and 1975, and released in 1975. For more information about TUTOR and PLATO, see the CYBIS tab. Dirk Pellett and Flint Pellett made substantial enhancements to the game from 1976 to 1985.

dnd was the first interactive game to feature what would later be referred to as "bosses."

In 1977, Daniel Lawrence rewrote dnd in BASIC (Beginners' All-purpose Symbolic Instruction Code) for the PDP-10 as DND. In 1982, he reworked and re-released the game as Telengard. In 1983, DEC (Digital Equipment Corporation, A.K.A. "Digital") rewrote DND in Pascal. Due to the availability of the BASIC source code, DND was later ported and adapted to newer systems and programming languages.

In 1984, Bill Knight published DND for DOS. In 1988, he retitled and re-released it as Dungeons of the Necromancer's Domain (which is the download available here).

dnd is the second-earliest surviving role-playing video game, behind only pedit5 (A.K.A. "The Dungeon"). pedit5 is believed to have been preceeded only by a game called "m199h," but no copies of that game survive. Some accounts claim that pedit5 may have actually preceeded m199h. dnd was directly inspired by pedit5, and most other early RPGs were directly inspired by dnd.

Doom

Download

Doom was designed by John Romero, Tom Hall, and Sandy Petersen, and developed by id Software in 1993 for MS-DOS.

Doom helped to define the FPS genre, and pioneered such technologies as 3D graphics, networked multiplayer, and modding.

In 1997, id Software released the game's source code. This, along with the 2.39 megabyte file size of the original game, led to enthusiasts porting the game to a variety of devices - many of which were never designed for gaming (such as an ATM). Hence the phrase, "it runs Doom."

Oubliette

Download

pedit5 is the earliest surviving RPG (preceeded only by the now-lost "m119h"). dnd was directly inspired by pedit5, and is the second-earliest surviving RPG. For more context on the early history of this game, see the DND tab. In 1975, Kevet Duncombe and Jim Battin developed Moria for the PLATO system. Moria was directly inspired by dnd - the developers claim not to have read J.R.R. Tolkien's works or known about Dungeons & Dragons when they began working on the game. Moria allowed players to play in groups of up to ten, and featured dynamically-generated dungeons and a wireframe first-person display. Oubliette was directly inspired by Moria.

Oubliette was developed by Jim Schwaiger, John Gaby, Bencherd DeLong, and Jerry Bucksath, and published on the PLATO system in 1977. For more information about PLATO, see the CYBIS tab. It was known for being so difficult that it was impossible to play alone.

This version was ported to DOS in 1983 by Bear Systems.

Spacewar!

Download

Spacewar! was developed by Steve Russell (later Martin Graetz, Wayne Wiitanen, Bob Saunders, and Steve Piner) in 1962 for the PDP-1 at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. It was later spread to several installations of the PDP-1 computer, making it the first video game to be played at multiple computers.

Spacewar! directly inspired the first two commercial arcade video games, Galaxy Game (1971) and Computer Space (1971), as well as Orbitwar (PLATO, 1974) and Asteroids (1979).

Spacewar! was the first game on the game canon at the Library of Congress.

The first-ever video game tournament was the "Intergalactic Spacewar! Olympics," held on October 19, 1972 and hosted by Rolling Stone.

This version was ported to DOS by Bill Seiler in 1985, and is very faithful to the original.

Wizardry was created by Andrew C. Greenberg and Robert Woodhead, and developed and published by Sir-Tech from 1981-2001. Only games one through five of the original series were released for DOS.

Wizardry (along with Ultima) was one of the main influencers of role-playing games. It was the direct influence for such titles as Final Fantasy and Dragon Quest. Wizardry's main influence was Oubliette (PLATO).

Zork was written between 1977 and 1979 using MDL (Model Development Language or More Datatypes than Lisp, A.K.A. "muddle"; a descendant of the LISP language) on a PDP-10 by Tim Anderson, Marc Blank, Bruce Daniels, and Deve Lebling of the MIT Dynamic Modeling Group.

When Zork was published comercially, it was split up into three games.

Zork is one of the earliest interactive fiction games, drawing roots directly from the original game of its genre, Adventure.
